Overview

Tencent, as a large Internet company, has been in a long-term collaboration with Dunhuang Academy, one of the major cultural heritage preservation organizations in China. The overall intention of this collaboration was to enhance the young public's awareness towards cultural heritage preservation, yet, past projects did not perform well at fundraising.

Our team at Tencent CDC (an internal design agency) thus hoped to experiment with something different, to bring donors closer to Dunhuang...

Key design objectives

Promote Donation

Digitalize concepts in traditional arts and culture

Fuse e-charity with e-commerce

Understand donors' motivations and reasons why they don't donate for cultural heritage preservation.

Explore ways charity can be presented digitally in the context of traditional culture given all uniqueness.

Explore ways charity can utilize e-commerce design tactics.

Design Outcomes

We designed Lightup Dunhuang, enabling users to virtually light up a unique piece of wall painting in Dunhuang cave temples upon each donation, through a spatial storytelling experience that created a strong sense of engagement.

1. Enter spatial narrative
2. Explore
3. Donate and get a candle
4. Light up and share

1. Enter the panoramic cave space, and follow the narrative that brings you back to a thousand years ago.

Rationales

Connecting people with the space keep users engaged with the cultural heritage site. Also, the construction of narrative helps to guide users through the journey and provides a sense of ritual.

2. Explore the cave space, the wallpaintings, the pieces that have been lighted up by others.

Rationales

Exploration is what lies between entry and donation, and it's the key in guiding new users to donate. Having the sense of community and better encourage users to join and be the next one to donate. The ritual atmosphere and the cave space both evoke respect for the cultural heritage, and guide users to better appreciate it.

3. As the guardian of the cave, donate different amount of money to light up a piece of wallpainting of different sizes.

Rationales

Donation is kept simple, clear, yet engaging to facilitate faster decision. Users get a good sense of what they will be getting in return of donation, but don't over-focus on the final result.

4. Upon donation, the candle flies to the wall and light up the wallpainting. A certificate and a personalized report will be generated for sharing.

Rationales

The process of lighting up wallpainting makes the experience consistent and complete. The rewards provide multiple ways for users to draw personal connection with the piece of wallpainting he/she lights up, making the experience satisfying that he/she would like to share.

To have a clearer picture who we're designing for throughout the project, I synthesized data that I gathered about potential users, discussed with the senior designer on the best target groups, and summarized them in persona.

Persona: The 2 types of potential donors

Insight: The lack of connection leads to the lack of motivation to donate

Relatability

"It doesn't matter to me"

This mainly happens to casual audience who haven't visited Dunhuang. Cultural heritage is by nature historically & geologically far away, involving large and serious topics that are hard to relate to.

Contribution

"I don't matter to it"

Unlike charity that helps real individuals out, it is unclear how one‚Äôs donation is going to make an impact to such a vague and large project, and the sense of contribution is thus low.

Perceived Rewards

"I'll be happier if I get something"

Traditional charity works one-way: it relies on donors giving away things selflessly. However, donors‚Äô motivation increases significantly when certain degree of rewards are provided.

Map out Journey

We mapped out the experience blueprint, from the starting touchpoint to sharing upon completion of donation. We paid attention to the user goals and emotion for each stage, in order to plan features and tasks to achieve both user goals and product goals, and to create a smoother and more complete experience.

Below are some key design decisions we made along with rationales behind when designing the 3 key stages: pre-donation, donation, and post-donation.

Pre-Donation

How to effectively build tension and atmosphere?

1. Construct concise yet engaging introduction

To increase the sense of engagement, it's important to design an emmersive opening experience with the use of storytelling. However, as we learned from previous products, it can't be long, or else users can be easily distracted and bored. We thus strived to make every screen count:

2. Build up the sense of engagement and urgency

To increase the sense of engagement, we wanted to make users feel they're part of something large. Also, creating the sense of urgency builds the tension.

3. Reduce interference in the main flow

To promote donation, we purposefully kept the overall flow short and focused. Viewing pieces lighted up by others builds users' motivation to donate, but users may also get easily interrupted from it.

Donation

How to help users make up their minds on the spot of donation?

Donation is the stage where users have to pause and think about carefully, as they are asked to pay real money. However, the situation is trickier than traditional e-commerce:

1. Visualize the reward of payment, yet not to make it too result-oriented

To encourage payment, we needed to give users some sense of what they're getting after the payment, but we can't put too much weight on the final reward:

2. Facilitate fast decision by reducing choices

From the past data of Tencent Charity, providing too many options with insignificant differences may cause decidophobia, which will lower users‚Äô willingess to make the payment. Thus, we want to get rid of unnecessary distraction:

Post-Donation

How to encourage users to share?

1. Design Goals and Experience Division

We divided the post-donation experience into 3 levels: completion, acknowledgement, and sharing. Through the progressive experience, users are satisfied on different levels, from forming an impressive donation experience to actually want to share what they get to their friends.

2. Sharing and Social Currency

When designing the sharable content, we thought about what will trigger users to share, and therefore we looked at the model of personal social currency. Considering features of the young population that we are serving, they are people who love showcasing their personality and identity when sharing things on social media, and they love talking about and comparing unique personalized results with friends. Therefore, we mainly focused on identity, conversation and valuable information when designing the values of the sharable content.

Testing & Iterations

Talking to users

Due to the confidentiality of this project, I was not allowed to show the idea to people outside of the organization, so I present the design to some colleagues that could be potential users, and here was some quotes I collected:

We learned that:

Is it worthy to donate?

Most participants found the idea intriguing and claimed to be more willing to donate. However, many were still very cautious with the actual payment and needed time to figure out if it's really worthy, particularly for users that are not super fans of traditional culture.

Offline trip and souvenir

Participants' willingness to donate significantly increase if they were to be shown this app during their trip to Dunhuang, and this opened new possibility of offline collaboration with Dunhuang.

Talking to developers and visual designers

As the interaction designer, I needed to communicate with visual designers and front-end developers to carry the project forward. The most important thing we discussed was the animation and atmosphere building. We discussed technical constraints (e.g. how to locate a piece of painting, how many pieces are there in a cave, etc.), as well as the ideal artstyle and user experience.

Reflection

With all fantastic designers from Tencent CDC

  • Impress your users quickly: The most special thing about this project is, the touchpoint between users and the product is extremely short, that it's almost like an advertisement to convince customers into making purchase right away. Therefore, although the flow may look short, careful effort was put into the choice and distillation of information and interactions. We strived to utilize the short amount of time to provoke the right emotion and motivation.
  • Don't make a product for the sake of the product: Particularly in big organizations, designers have to think about the meaning of the product from a larger landscape: What values can this little product bring to the larger project? How may it affect the project/team/company from a long run? What type of image does it create for the company for audience to see?
  • Not everyone is going to like your design, and that‚Äôs totally fine: I was a bit frustrated when I received more feedback saying they probably won‚Äôt donate than I expected. I then came to realize that especially for a B2C product, it‚Äôs simply impossible to design something that caters to everyone‚Äôs taste and need. My mentor told me that phenomenal products were very rare, but if I manage to make 1% more people donate and 5% more people aware of the issue (of cultural heritage preservation), it‚Äôs considered a great success!
