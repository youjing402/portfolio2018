Light Up Dunhuang

Experimenting the crossover between philanthropy and e-commerce: A creative e-philanthropy product that engages the young public to donate for traditional cultural heritage protection.

Overview

Tencent, as a large Internet company, has been in a long-term collaboration with Dunhuang Academy, one of the major cultural heritage protection organizations in China. The overall intention of this collaboration was to enhance the young public's awareness towards cultural heritage protection, yet, past projects did not perform well at fundraising.

The team thus hoped to explore something different, to bring donors closer to Dunhuang...

Key design objectives

Promote Donation

Digitalize concepts in traditional arts and culture

Fuse e-philanthropy with e-commerce

Understand donors' motivations and reasons why they don't donate for cultural heritage protection.

Explore ways philanthropy can be presented digitally in the context of traditional culture given all uniqueness.

Explore ways philanthropy can utilize e-commerce design tactics.

Design Outcome

We designed Lightup Dunhuang, enabling users to virtually light up a unique piece of wall painting in Dunhuang cave temples upon each donation, through a spatial storytelling experience that created a strong sense of engagement.

1. Enter spatial narrative
2. Explore
3. Donate and get a candle
4. Light up and share

1. Enter the panoramic cave space, and follow the narrative that brings you back to a thousand years ago.

Rationales

Connecting people with the space keep users engaged with the cultural heritage site. Also, the construction of narrative helps to guide users through the journey and provides a sense of ritual.

Challenge 1 - Understanding donors

Who are the target users? Why do or don't they donate? How to motivate them?

To have a clearer picture who we're designing for throughout the project, I synthesized data that I gathered about potential users, discussed with the senior designer on the best target groups, and summarized them in persona.

Persona: The 2 types of potential donors

Insight: The lack of connection leads to the lack of motivation to donate

Relatability

"It doesn't matter to me"

This mainly happens to casual audience who haven't visited Dunhuang. Cultural heritage is by nature historically & geologically far away, involving large and serious topics that are hard to relate to.

Contribution

"I don't matter to it"

Unlike charity that helps real individuals out, it is unclear how one’s donation is going to make an impact to such a vague and large project, and the sense of contribution is thus low.

Perceived Rewards

"I'll be happier if I get something"

Traditional charity works one-way: it relies on donors giving away things selflessly. However, donors’ motivation increases significantly when certain degree of rewards are provided.

Challenge 2 - Past Product Review

What made existing charity products failed or successful?

We examined several existing online charity products in the market to learn about the general pattern, features that work well or not well under different context and topics.

The analysis mainly focused on examining solutions for building donor’s motivation based on the previous 3 dimensions: Relatability, contribution, and rewards. For projects that have data available, we also looked at the rough data (e.g. number of sharing, donation, etc.).

Some e-charity products that we reviewed

We learned that:

✖️ Overemphasis on atmosphere building

Many products we reviewed focused too much on building atmosphere through storytelling. It's good for raising awareness, but the call for donation could be easily ignored by users.

✔️ Introduction of real-world values and connection

The sense of engagement in charity could be drastically enhanced through personalized rewards and real world impact.

A popular environmental protection charity app planted a tree in real world when users planted a virtual one

How to find the right thing to connect with donors in the right form?

Right Connection

I researched Dunhuang arts and architecture and brainstormed a list of its sub-elements along with possible ways that the audience may connect with them.

Right Form

In order to find the right form/metaphor to present the idea, we had to fulfill Dunhuang Academy's needs: to respect the artwork and make it less religious, and users' needs: to be easy to understand and relate to.

Among all combinations, individuals collecting painting fragments is the one that establishes the strongest personal bond.

One of our ideas turned down by Dunhuang Academy because it didn't show respect: it explicitly conveyed the idea of cutting through artwork and bringing a piece home.

Reflection

  • Think ahead and design for more flexibility: As I joined the team at an early stage, it was an important job for me to learn to prioritize features with the team while also keeping an eye on what may possibly come next (e.g. marketing campaign) in order to design in a more flexible way to accommodate future changes.
  • Find alternative ways to deal with challenging user groups: Children are surely one of the most unique and challenging user groups to conduct research on. To approach the problem differently, I tried to get more information and knowledge via reading and talking to professional educators and parents about their first-hand experience. They sometimes were able to explain children's behaviors and mentality even better than children themselves, which helped me greatly in understanding what I needed to design.
