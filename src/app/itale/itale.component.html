Overview

Storytelling is a fun educational exercise that boosts children's imagination and literacy skills. When storytelling education shifts from classroom to home, new opportunities and issues emerge.

On one hand, at-home storytelling comes with more flexibility and fun; on the other hand, without professional guidance, it's harder for children to effectively hone and broaden their storytelling skill sets.

Key design challenges

Product-wise: How to coordinate children's needs for fun experience and parents' expectations for visible educational values?

Experience-wise: How to help children overcome challenges, pick up key creative storytelling skills, and most importantly, enjoy the experience?

Solution Overview

A story playground enabling children to use illustration pieces of story elements to freely act out and record stories. Story frameworks and during-story guidance are integrated to help children get a better grasp of narrative flow and sparkle their creativity.

Challenge 1 - User Needs & Product Directions

What do children and parents value, and how to coordinate their different needs?

We conducted interview with parents on their first-hand experience, and consulted education experts on children's mental development (to read more about research methods, click here).

What did we do?

Mapping User Goals

We synthesized research results by mapping out children’s and parents’ key motivations, goals, and beliefs on storytelling. We also summarized potential educational values of storytelling we learned from educators.

Finding Common Theme

To help us decide the overall product design direction, we had parents prioritize things they care about about storytelling education, and synthesized them with what children care about the most:

What did we learn and decide?

Fun > Education

Children focus primarily on the fun aspect and require emotional motives to stay engaged; Adults love to see visible long-term educational values and potential, along with the end result of children’s work.

Creativity > Hard Skills

Being carefree and creative is why children enjoy storytelling, and creativity is among the top lists of educational values that parents hope children to gain through storytelling.

Individual use > Family use

Although most parents agreed that storytelling is important to family communication, many also admitted they don't always have time and energy to guide children through the storytelling process.

[Implication] We want to make sure the product is enjoyable in the first place, that children want to use it out of their own will, but also fulfills its educational goal.

[Implication] Although other hard skills are also important to cover, story creativity will be the top focus of the product.

[Implication] It's best for the product to primarily target children's individual use at home.

Challenge 2 - Experience & Education

What are problems and opportunities in the current storytelling experience?

We conducted interviews with parents and teachers about their observation and children's experience, collected stories created by children, and read about existing storytelling educational methods to understand the current storytelling experience and emotions behind.

What did we do?

Comparing different storytelling forms

To get a more comprehensive understanding of what makes a good storytelling experience in general, we looked into different types of common storytelling practices that children have, and compared their attributes.

Comparing different storytelling products

To examine what major competitors in the market were doing, we picked 3 products to analyze features in terms of experience and education results to understand what worked and what didn't.

Going deep into the intended scenario

We further investigated the intended scenario (i.e. at-home individual storytelling) in detail and spot issues/opportunities in terms of educational values and fun experience:

What did we learn and decide?

Freedom sparkles creativity, but that's not enough

Creativity is not always about inventing everything from scratch, but more about getting inspired and innovating. Proper amount of guidance (e.g. framework, prompts, etc.) greatly helps children expand imagination and organize their creative ideas.

Children lack awareness of story flow and development

When telling stories on their own, many tend to go on and on without much overall control and focus on development (both depth and width). Once they get stuck, they lose interest easily, leaving many dimensions unexplored.

Communication spices up the experience

Children love to share their stories with others. Conversations give them a sense of engagement, and they get more encouraged when being listened to and reacted by the audience actively.

[Implication] Ensure enough freedom and provide guidance when needed

[Implication] Provide both overall guidance and during-storytelling inspiration

[Implication] Make the guidance conversation-like and provide social support

MVP Strategy

For the MVP, we focused on the design of core storytelling mechanisms first. We learned that children need free space to experiment on their own, while being guided effectively both before and during storytelling. Therefore, we brainstormed and developed the basic storytelling tactic of building blocks + streetlights:

Building Blocks

Building blocks are basic elements used to construct the story. Its primary goal here is for children to comprehend story elements and comfortably brainstorm plots.

We brainstormed different ways to construct a story after assessing common storytelling practices that children adopted and various storytelling techniques on how a story is presented to children in an educational setting.

To ensure both fun experience and educational values, we decided to go with visualizing Who-Where-What elements, and children can drag elements around and act out the story:

Streetlights

Streetlights are guidance throughout the process that helps children think about stories more thoroughly and creatively. Also, they are inspiration that keeps children excited prevents them from running out of ideas.

As the research showed, children lack overall control of storyflow and the skill to properly develop plot. Therefore, we decided to include both overall planning guidance and on-the-way guidance.

Reflection

  • Think ahead and design for more flexibility: As I joined the team at an early stage, it was an important job for me to learn to prioritize features with the team while also keeping an eye on what may possibly come next (e.g. marketing campaign) in order to design in a more flexible way to accommodate future changes.
  • Find alternative ways to deal with challenging user groups: Children are surely one of the most unique and challenging user groups to conduct research on. To approach the problem differently, I tried to get more information and knowledge via reading and talking to professional educators and parents about their first-hand experience. They sometimes were able to explain children's behaviors and mentality even better than children themselves, which helped me greatly in understanding what I needed to design.
