Background

Rhythm games are full of pop music, but we hope to convey the message that classical pieces can be very different from the typical stereotype: sometimes they are elegant and peaceful, yet, at other times, they can as well be powerful and exciting.

We aimed at presenting different sides of classical music to young audience through a VR rhythm game with the fusion of music + visual + motion in an immersive environment.

Challenges

  • How to Engage with Young Audience?

    Young audience often don't know much about and don't have special interests for classical music. The design needs to be engaging in the sense that the audience can enjoy the game and develop interests for learning more without much prior knowoledge. The choice of music and the way to present it would be critical.

  • How to Embody Abstract Music Experience?

    Classical music is often abstract with no lyrics presented. Therefore, bringing it to life with the aid of appropriate visual images and context is important.

  • How to Create Immersive Experience?

    Immersive experience is crucial to engaging music experience. We hope to design for ways to better combine music, visuals, and motion using VR technology to maximize player's senses to make the experience more memorable and natural.

Outcomes

Key Features

8 classical pieces with diverse styles represented by nature images

To encourage audience to engage with classical music, we conducted audience study and selected pieces in a mixture of both popular and less popular ones, and with diverse and distinct charateristics that can be easily pictured.

Terrain design to embody music emotion and experience

To embody abstract music experience, we mapped out music atmosphere using nature images of 4 seasons and day/night, conducted secondary research and brainstormed to create suitable visual terrain representation for each piece.

Engaging motion combined with music flow and beats

The core game mechanics is straightforward and engaging. Music, visual, and body motion are combined in the sense that in the scene, players move with music to hit rhythm, enjoying the holistic experience.

Level mechanism for higher playability

Multiple levels are set to make the game worth more experience and keep players engaged. Also, by unlocking levels, different instruments will be unlocked for players to explore more ways to have fun.

Sample Gameplay

In the game, players are flying forward, with music notes placed on the pathway according to the music flow. By feeling the music rhythm, players can easily move hands and body to hit rhythm notes.

Game Levels

There are in total of 8 stages of 8 pieces of classical music. They are mapped to 4 seasons and daytime/night according to their impression and stories behind. Levels are to be unlocked one after one, and instruments are unlocked along with levels for players to explore different ways to perform music.

How we got here

The overview

Timeline

Envisioning interaction forms

Conceptualization

We want the experience to be engaging and intuitive, and thus, having multiple interaction design plans, We chose it to be straightforward, so that even rookies can easily enjoy the simple game control.

Researching & mapping classical music

Music Selection

From examining 50+ pieces of classical music and talking to college students about what their perception and preferences of classical music, we selected a diverse set of 8 pieces based on factors such as popularity, rhythm and styles, we finalized a list of eight pieces of music of different composers, with two (easier and harder) for each season.

High-level flow

Game Flow

Having the basic stage setting, we designed the overall game flow of level system and instrument system. In the design, we took care of both casual players and more hard-core players who wish to challenge for more surprises.

We allowed players to unlock the next season as a whole just by clearing the previous easier song (as demonstrated below). For hard stages, they were “extra” stages that were rewarding yet non-compulsory: by clearing those stages, players would be able to unlock different instruments to experience different performance styles.

Theming music and stages

Stage Concepts

Having the list of music, we then started to design the context and component of stages. Every stage was designed based on the music. Components include level design, beat map design, motion design, and mood.

Case study of designing everything within a stage

Level Design

Stage 2-1 (Summer Day – Alla Turca) would be taken as the example for case study below. Midi file of the piece was analyzed section by section according to moods and speed. Keywords are extracted and used to brainstorm the features and flow of terrain.

Basic terrain along with flying route was built in Unity according to the specification and drafts. After testing in VR, we adjusted the length and height of the route, and adjusted each sub-terrain according to the actual timing and tones of each section of the music.

Thereafter, visual and lightings were polished based on the uniform game theme and specific music theme. Alla Turca was a brisk song in summer, and thus the overall visual style was bright with blue-green tone to create a cool-down yet vigorous atmosphere. Details such as grass, trees, stones and other decorations were added.

We integrated the beat map into the terrain and adjust their positions and speed through testing in VR. We made sure that notes were within the appropriate range for players to hit and that movements were at the right difficulty level.

I then design the visual aspect of in-game interaction: including the shape, size and appearance of notes, as well as controller model, animation and interaction with notes. We tested in VR to ensure that the interaction was smooth and that players felt in control.

Usability and playability

Testing & Iteration

Testing was done concurrently with development. We had both inner group and outer group testing.

1/ Auto View-angle Adjustment

Previously, the orientation and viewing angle in VR was set completely flexible. This was because we wanted players to have more freedom to control their viewing angles. However, this did not work well when testing: sometimes when route was curving or precipitous, players could easily get lost and they were not given enough time to react promptly to the changing directions.

Thus, we implemented the auto orientation adjustment so that players’ horizontal line of sight would always be the tangent of the route. By doing so, although players might lose some control, it became easier for them to follow the beat route.

2/ Motion Adjustment

We adjusted the hitting distance of a note for several times, taking into account human’s arm length, reaction time and moving speed so as to create a more accurate match between music and motion.

However, when route was precipitous, extra work was needed to adjust note position. For example, we observed that when players were flying downward with the waterfall, players tended to stretch out their hands horizontally while squatting, but still could not reach notes. Additionally, they reported dizziness when traveling up and down fast. To improve the experience, we made the falling slope less abrupt and adjusted the position of notes to be nearer to players.

3/ Controller Adjustment

We tested several controller feedback design, to see which ones gave a better feedback and interaction experience, and gathered feedback from players before we decided to go with the last design.

Reflection

It was a really special 3-month experience for me to get a taste of VR design and development, and I enjoyed leveraging my creativity to fuse classical music with visual and motion.

  • Explore and experiment with technology: Although I also read about VR experience and interface design online, a large portion of this project was done through iterative exploration and testing because there are not too many VR guidelines currently. I wished to learn some more professional approaches and frameworks to deal with VR design.
  • Design in 3D Space: Designing in 3D space is very different and actually harder than on 2D-plane, especially when the design needs to be immersive: it becomes harder to mock up, harder to imagine and draft out everything using pen and paper, and needs to be tested in the real 3D environment frequently to make sure everything works fine.
