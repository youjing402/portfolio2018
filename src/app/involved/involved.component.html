Prompt

A new school year is approaching and the orientation team is looking to you for some design expertise. Design an experience for new students to browse, search, and propose new student organizations.

Problems

New students' overall goal is to find suitable organizations to engage with.

Exploring existing organizations and proposing new organizations are 2 ways going about accomplishing this goal. And they both have certain problems:

Exploration is Inefficient

Currently, there are mainly 2 ways of exploring and examining existing oganizations: "yellow pages" (e.g. school organization website, involvement fair) and word of mouth.

Students find yellow pages not supporting efficient exploration and do not provide reliable information that they need. Although word of mouth is reliable and more tailored, it's still not efficient as it can be hard to find someone with the right interpersonal connections and know the right information.

Proposal lacks support

Only a few students choose to start new organizations. Aside from the objective factor that they prefer to join pre-created community, the high perceived burden of starting application and the lack of recruitment support further impede the new proposal, that it's hard to establish the community with right people and idea.

Solution

Interpersonal Connection is the key to my solution.

Exploration tailoring based on friend's interests

Within the personal interested categories, the ones that are most popular among friends will be prioritized in the exploration list.

Rationales

From interviews, students tend to be more inclined to knowing and joining organizations that people they know have joined. It provides a way to quickly prioritize options that are more likely to interest users from dozens of others, which makes the exploration process efficient and more accurate.

Conversation for authentic and personal evaluation

When viewing an organization, users will see friends who are members and start conversation with them for more detailed information.

Rationales

From interviews, students are looking for information related to personal experience which most organization official pages do not provide, and they generally don't want to ask strangers at first place. By letting them quickly see friends in there they can talk to, it makes the process of getting to know an organization holistically more efficient and less stressful.

Crowdsourcing for recruitment and idea polishment

When proposing new organizations, ideas don't need to be finalized and can be posted to match people with same interests and start recruitment and idea iterations. People will be able to see proposals when they search for relevant keywords and react.

Rationales

From officer interview and task flow analysis of the new organization registration system, I discovered that all materials and initial recruitment need to be finalized before students enter the proposal system. By providing such preparation stage, the perceived barrier of proposing lowers down and student get support from the larger community.

Process

Starting & Planning

Research Goals

Orientation events and student organization culture in different universities can be quite different. To familiarize myself with how these work in IU, after doing some secondary research online, I decided to conduct some quick field studies with students and staff members of relevant programs at IU to gain a complete picture.

Through the research, I want to learn:

  • Why do new students join and propose organizations and what are their goals?
  • What is the current process, platforms, and experience of joining and proposing organizations?
  • What matter the most to new students when looking for organizations?

The Big Picture

Why New Students Joining Organizations is Important

Talking to both students and staff members helped me gain a larger picture of the importance of new students joining organizations, not just to students themselves, but also to organizations and school:

Synthesizing goals of different stakeholders helped me locate the most important goal for three of them in common, which is to enable students to discover and engage with the community that is suitable for them.

"Engagement with community is physically, emotionally, and culturally."

However, finding a community/organization and fit in is not always easy.

Overall Flow

How New Students Attempt to Find a Suitable Organization

Synthesizing results from secondary research of existing platform at IU and from interviews with students, I mapped out the existing flow of joining organizations and proposing new organizations, the 2 ways of engaging with organization.

According to the resesarch, most new students would not consider establishing new organizations, so I separate the 2 paths and discuss them separately. (Steps with low saturation are out of scope of this project, yet they are shown to complete the overall picture of the flow.)

Explore & Evaluate

Based on research, before deciding to join organizations, students will go through 2 stages/steps to help them "diverge and converge":

  1. to first explore openly for potential organizations that may interest and fit them.
  2. to evaluate options carefully regarding whether they will be good fits by learning more detailed information.

Existing Channels & Insights

To further explore how new students experience with such exploration and evaluation, I mapped out what they do and what they think in different stages, discover things they care when looking for suitable organizations, what problems they have and why they are not achieving the desired results through the current channels.

Insights - What Information are Students Looking for

To determine what organizations to look for and whether they are "the right one", students are often directed by the following 2 factors:

Interest: What They Do

Whether personal interest matches what organizations do is often the first thing students consider: vague or specific, they have something in mind, and would first look for organizations cater to their intersts.

Culture: Who They Are

The culture fostered and the interpersonal connections in organizations are important to know. Organizations doing similar things can have different styles and atmospheres, and students want to make sure they find the right one.

Problems - Current Channels of Acquiring Information

The map further reveals insights of preferences and problems when students are experiencing with the current channels of gaining the most important information as mentioned above. Students are:

Tired of overwhelming information with no personal tailoring

"No one is looking at that long list of organizations on the website or the fair handouts: it’s just too overwhelming with irrelevant information that doesn't know my interest."

"It's ineffective to explore potential activities: if I am to explore the category of arts, all the website shows me is the list of 135 organizations sorted alphabetically and I have to go through them to discover what kinds of activitie are in there."

Comfortable with knowing an organization from people, yet asking everyone individually can be a burden

"I prefer hearing from friends and knowing what they are up to. But you just can't get to everone."

"Before joining, I’d like to know more about the kind of culture in the organization. Offcial sites say little about it and do not seem as reliable. So I often ask friends or acquaintance who know that organization. But sometimes it take time to figure out who I can ask."

What If the experience of looking for organizations is...

Tailored by personal interests and social connections?
Reliable & Authentic that one can explore and know more about organization from friends and member-generated information?
Supportive of Interest Exploration that students can easily discover new things under interested categories?

Ideation

From the insights, I brainstormed the concept with core features of:

The underlying core idea is that social connection and word of mouth are being emphasized. New students can see friends' organizations, and who are or were in an interested organization and take their words as reference to help them decide whether it is a good fit for them.

There are mainly 2 ways for students to explore potential interested organizations: (1) the traditional searching and browsing via categories or keywords with support for easier exploration, (2) browse what friends are into. What students can see via both ways are tailored by their personal interests.

Paper Prototyping & Testing

I asked participants to go through the 3 tasks with the think-out-loud technique, observed their behaviors, and collected feedback for improvement.

  • Participants want to know what kinds of topics can be covered under categories in order to help them make decision whether they indeed cater to their preferences. Therefore sub-categories are added.

  • Participant are excited about the mechanism of getting to know what friends are up to, yet they mostly want to keep their own preferences prioritized with friend preferences as references. Thus, order feature is cancelled and the searching result is ranked by the default order where personal interest is the primary factor followed by friend popularity.

Who, When, and How

Context of Proposing New Organizations

Based on interviews, most new students would not consider establishing new organizations because:

  1. As new students, they are not yet familiar with things on campus: don't know many people that have the same interest and want to start an organization from scratch, and are not familiar with the campus organization system.
  2. They don't think there it does not worth time and efforts to create a new community if there is already one that is not bad to fit in.

I also talked to staff at the IU Involvment Center and experienced with the flow of the existing system at IU of new organization application and found the following issues:

Synthesizing findings from both participants and existing platform analysis, I concluded the following challenges to tackle:

High perceived burden to start & Lack of Support for unmatured proposals

The current system demands everything to be fully prepared by students before they start applying for organization regristration and get rejected for several times. The perceived burden is too high at the beginning, and unmatured proposals are not getting timely feedback.

Lack support for recruitment and peers matching

Recruiting members is not easy for new organizations and new students. Promotion channel is limited: either by nudging friends, or physically promoting within small range. It also does not guarantee that members recruited have the same goal and commitment.

Ideation: Crowdsourcing the Proposal

The design of new proposal experience aimed at making the preparation process (including idea polishing and member recruitment) of a new organization proposal easier by "crowdsourcing": opening up incomplete proposals to public and get support until it's fully prepared and all the subsequent documents sent for offcials to review.

I hoped that through this design, it is less stressful for new students to propose an idea, and they can easily include people who match here and better fund a community that people with the same goals can fit in.

User Flow

Designing Proposing Experience

Based on the idea from insights, I developed the flow for user to (1) Propose organization to public and receive recruitment help and idea suggestion from other users looking for it; (2) Naturally browse through proposal list and start new proposal when they can't find satisfacting organizations.

Paper Prototype & Testing

  • The onboarding instruction for creating proposal is not clear and not intuitive. Steps need to be clear enough for people to know what are to be done by themselves and what are to be done by the platform.

  • Comparing with the description of established organizations, participants would like to see more unique things about vision and promises from the proposed organizations. Therefore, the information to be entered and the instruction are changed.

Research Methods

Reflection

  • Do just-enough research: The amount of research can be done is always restricted by time. Given such a short time frame of 7 days, I could have spent more time doing the actual design, and be more comfortable with knowing only "a bit" of things.
  • Divide complex tasks into pieces: Although this is a small project, it still involves multiple tasks which are all not easy to think through and solve neatly (i.e. the browsing part and the proposal part). I previously intended to tie the two together to find if there's a way to perfectly connect the two, but it turn out that divide and conquer is the key. Sometimes it worths thinking to what degree things should be connected, in order to stop & separate at the right moment.
